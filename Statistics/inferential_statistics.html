Topic One

Central Limit Theorem

What is CLT?

The Central Limit Theorem (CLT) is arguably the most important theorem in all of statistics. It states that when you take repeated random samples of size n from ANY population (regardless of its shape — skewed, uniform, bimodal, whatever), the distribution of the sample means will approach a normal distribution as n becomes large enough.

This is revolutionary because it means we can use the normal distribution as an approximation in almost all real-world problems, even when the underlying data is not normally distributed.

Central Limit Theorem — Formal Statement
X̄ ~ N(μ, σ²/n)

Where:
· X̄ = Sample mean
· μ = Population mean (mean of sampling distribution)
· σ = Population standard deviation
· n = Sample size
· σ/√n = Standard Error of the mean

Rule of thumb: CLT kicks in when n ≥ 30

Intuition: Why Does CLT Work?

Imagine rolling a die (uniform distribution — each value 1–6 equally likely). If you roll it once, the result is uniform. But if you roll it 30 times and take the average, and repeat that experiment thousands of times, those averages form a bell curve. The extreme values cancel each other out, and means cluster around the true average. This "averaging out" effect is the essence of CLT.

Visual: Sampling Distribution Approaches Normal

As sample size increases, the distribution of sample means becomes increasingly bell-shaped (normal), regardless of the original population distribution.

Step-by-Step Manual Example

Scenario A factory produces bolts. The diameter (mm) of the entire population follows a right-skewed distribution with μ = 10mm and σ = 2mm. We take 100 samples, each of size n = 36. What does the sampling distribution look like?

Identify population parameters
Population Mean (μ) = 10 mm | Population SD (σ) = 2 mm | n = 36
Check CLT condition
n = 36 ≥ 30 ✓ → CLT is applicable even though population is skewed
Determine the mean of the sampling distribution
μ(X̄) = μ = 10 mm
Calculate Standard Error (SE)
SE = σ/√n = 2/√36 = 2/6 = 0.333 mm
Conclude about sampling distribution
X̄ ~ N(μ = 10, SE = 0.333) → Approximately Normal despite skewed population
Find probability that sample mean is between 9.67 and 10.33 mm
Z₁ = (9.67 − 10) / 0.333 = −0.33/0.333 = −1.0
Z₂ = (10.33 − 10) / 0.333 = 0.33/0.333 = +1.0
P(−1 ≤ Z ≤ 1) = 0.6827 → 68.27% of sample means fall here

Key Properties of CLT

📊
Shape

Sampling distribution of X̄ is approximately Normal for large n, regardless of population shape

🎯
Center

Mean of the sampling distribution equals the population mean: μ(X̄) = μ

📏
Spread

Standard deviation of sampling distribution (SE) = σ/√n. Larger n → smaller SE → more precise

🔢
Sample Size

For normally distributed populations, any n works. For non-normal, use n ≥ 30

Applications of CLT

🏭

🗳️

💊
PharmaceuticalsAverage drug efficacy across patient samples
💰

🌾

📡

When to Use / When NOT to Use

✓ Use CLT When
  • Sample size n ≥ 30
  • Random, independent samples
  • Population mean & SD are known
  • Making inferences about sample means
  • Building hypothesis tests or CI for means
  • Population can be any shape
✗ Avoid CLT When
  • n < 30 AND population is highly skewed
  • Samples are NOT independent
  • Working with medians, not means
  • Population has infinite variance
  • Data has extreme outliers dominating
  • Working with proportions (use different formula)

Topic Two

Standard Error

What is Standard Error?

People often confuse Standard Deviation (SD) and Standard Error (SE). Here's the key distinction:

  • Standard Deviation measures the spread of individual observations in a dataset — how much does a single data point vary from the mean?
  • Standard Error measures the spread of sample means — if you took many samples and computed their means, how much would those means vary?

SE quantifies the precision of your estimate. A small SE means your sample mean is a reliable estimate of the population mean. A large SE means there is more uncertainty.

Standard Error Formulas
SE(X̄) = σ / √n
When population σ is unknown (use sample s):
SE(X̄) = s / √n
Standard Error of Proportion:
SE(p̂) = √[ p̂(1−p̂) / n ]

Where: σ = population SD | s = sample SD | n = sample size | p̂ = sample proportion

Visual: Effect of Sample Size on Standard Error

As sample size n increases, Standard Error decreases (SE ∝ 1/√n). Doubling n reduces SE by factor of √2 ≈ 1.41.

SD vs SE — Clear Comparison

AspectStandard Deviation (SD)Standard Error (SE)
MeasuresSpread of individual data pointsSpread of sample means
Decreases with larger n?No — reflects actual data variabilityYes — SE = σ/√n
Use caseDescribing data distributionEstimating population parameter precision
Formula√[Σ(xᵢ−x̄)²/(n−1)]s/√n

Step-by-Step Manual Calculation

Example A teacher wants to estimate the average test score of 500 students. She samples 25 students and gets these scores:

72, 85, 90, 68, 77, 83, 91, 65, 78, 88, 74, 80, 69, 95, 72, 86, 79, 83, 71, 87, 76, 82, 70, 89, 74

Calculate Sample Mean (x̄)
Sum = 72+85+90+68+77+83+91+65+78+88+74+80+69+95+72+86+79+83+71+87+76+82+70+89+74
Sum = 1,973
x̄ = 1,973 / 25 = 78.92
Calculate deviations squared (xᵢ − x̄)²
(72−78.92)² = (−6.92)² = 47.89 | (85−78.92)² = (6.08)² = 36.97
(90−78.92)² = (11.08)² = 122.77 | (68−78.92)² = (−10.92)² = 119.25
... (continuing for all 25 values)
Σ(xᵢ − x̄)² ≈ 1,624.16
Calculate Sample Standard Deviation (s)
s² = Σ(xᵢ − x̄)² / (n−1) = 1,624.16 / 24 = 67.67
s = √67.67 = 8.23
Calculate Standard Error
SE = s / √n = 8.23 / √25 = 8.23 / 5 = 1.646
Interpret the result
SE = 1.646 means: if we took many samples of size 25, the sample means would
typically vary by about ±1.646 points from the true population mean (78.92)
💡 Key Insight: If we had used n = 100 instead of 25, SE would be: 8.23/√100 = 8.23/10 = 0.823 — half as large! Quadrupling the sample size halves the SE.

🩺

📈

🤖

🔬

When to Use / When NOT to Use

✓ Use SE When
  • Estimating precision of sample mean
  • Building confidence intervals
  • Comparing means across groups
  • Reporting uncertainty in averages
  • Conducting hypothesis tests on means
  • Drawing error bars in charts
✗ Don't Use SE When
  • Describing spread of individual data (use SD)
  • Reporting variability within a dataset
  • Comparing individual observations
  • Describing clinical range of values
  • Data is not randomly sampled

Topic Three

Point Estimation

What is Point Estimation?

In statistics, we rarely have access to an entire population. Instead, we use estimators — formulas applied to sample data — to guess the true value of a population parameter. A point estimate is a single value used to estimate an unknown population parameter.

Think of it like this: you want to know the average height of all adults in India (a population parameter). You can't measure everyone, so you measure a sample and use x̄ (the sample mean) as your best single guess for μ (the population mean). That single guess is a point estimate.

Common Point Estimators
Population ParameterPoint EstimatorFormula
Mean (μ)Sample Mean (x̄)x̄ = Σxᵢ / n
Variance (σ²)Sample Variance (s²)s² = Σ(xᵢ−x̄)² / (n−1)
Proportion (p)Sample Proportion (p̂)p̂ = x / n
Std Dev (σ)Sample Std Dev (s)s = √[Σ(xᵢ−x̄)²/(n−1)]
Maximum (θ)Sample Maximumx(max)

Properties of a Good Estimator

🎯
Unbiasedness

Expected value of the estimator equals the true parameter: E(x̄) = μ. The sample mean is an unbiased estimator of population mean. Note: sample variance uses (n-1), not n, to be unbiased.

📏
Efficiency

Among all unbiased estimators, the one with the smallest variance is called "efficient." The sample mean is the most efficient estimator for the population mean.

🔁
Consistency

As n → ∞, the estimator converges to the true parameter. Larger samples give estimates closer to the truth.

⚡
Sufficiency

The estimator uses all the information in the sample. No other function of the data provides additional information about the parameter.

Step-by-Step Manual Examples

Example 1: Estimating Population Mean

Problem A company wants to estimate the average number of customer complaints per day. They record complaints for 10 random days:

12, 18, 9, 22, 15, 11, 25, 14, 17, 20

Calculate Point Estimate for Mean (x̄)
Σxᵢ = 12+18+9+22+15+11+25+14+17+20 = 163
x̄ = 163 / 10 = 16.3 complaints/day
Calculate Point Estimate for Variance (s²)
Deviations: (12−16.3)²=18.49, (18−16.3)²=2.89, (9−16.3)²=53.29,
(22−16.3)²=32.49, (15−16.3)²=1.69, (11−16.3)²=28.09,
(25−16.3)²=75.69, (14−16.3)²=5.29, (17−16.3)²=0.49, (20−16.3)²=13.69
Σ(xᵢ−x̄)² = 232.1
s² = 232.1 / (10−1) = 232.1 / 9 = 25.79
Calculate Standard Deviation
s = √25.79 = 5.08 complaints/day
Interpret
Point Estimate of population mean μ = x̄ = 16.3
Point Estimate of population SD σ = s = 5.08
Limitation: This single value gives no idea of how accurate the estimate is → use CI for that

Example 2: Estimating Population Proportion

Problem Out of 200 surveyed customers, 130 said they were satisfied. What is the point estimate for the proportion of all satisfied customers?

Identify values
n = 200 (sample size) | x = 130 (satisfied customers)
Calculate sample proportion
p̂ = x / n = 130 / 200 = 0.65 = 65%
Interpret
Best single estimate: 65% of all customers are satisfied.
This is our point estimate for true population proportion p.

Visual: Point Estimate vs True Parameter

Each dot represents a sample mean from a different sample. The red line is the true population mean. Point estimates scatter around it — sometimes close, sometimes far.

Limitation of Point Estimates

⚠️ Critical Limitation: A point estimate gives us a single best guess, but tells us nothing about the uncertainty or accuracy of that guess. Two different samples might give point estimates of 78 and 82 — both valid, but which is closer to the truth? This is why we need Interval Estimation (Confidence Intervals).

📊

⚙️

When to Use / When NOT to Use

✓ Use Point Estimation When
  • Need a quick single-value summary
  • Decision requires a specific number
  • Reporting overview to non-technical audience
  • Sample size is very large (small SE anyway)
  • Preliminary analysis / exploration
  • Input to further calculations
✗ Avoid Point Estimation Alone When
  • Need to know uncertainty of estimate
  • Making high-stakes decisions (use CI)
  • Small sample size (high variability)
  • Comparing two populations
  • Regulatory or scientific reporting
  • Communicating precision matters

Topic Four

Interval Estimation
(Confidence Intervals)

What is a Confidence Interval?

A Confidence Interval (CI) is a range of values that is likely to contain the true population parameter with a specified probability (confidence level). Instead of saying "the mean is 50," we say "we are 95% confident the mean is between 47 and 53."

⚠️ Common Misconception: A 95% CI does NOT mean "there is a 95% chance that μ lies in this interval." The population mean μ is fixed (not random). The correct interpretation: "If we repeated this study 100 times and built a CI each time, about 95 of those 100 intervals would contain the true μ."
Confidence Interval Formulas
For Population Mean (σ known) — Z-interval:
CI = x̄ ± Z(α/2) × (σ/√n)
For Population Mean (σ unknown) — t-interval:
CI = x̄ ± t(α/2, n-1) × (s/√n)
For Population Proportion:
CI = p̂ ± Z(α/2) × √[p̂(1−p̂)/n]

Critical Z-values: 90% CI → Z = 1.645 | 95% CI → Z = 1.960 | 99% CI → Z = 2.576
Margin of Error (ME) = Z(α/2) × SE

Visual: Confidence Interval Interpretation (100 Samples)

Each horizontal bar is a 95% CI from a different sample. Green bars contain the true mean (red vertical line). Red bars miss it. ~95% of CIs capture the true parameter.

Step-by-Step Examples

Example 1: 95% CI for Mean (σ known)

Problem A hospital wants to estimate average patient recovery time. Population σ = 3.2 days (known from historical data). Sample of n = 64 patients gives x̄ = 8.5 days. Calculate 95% CI.

Identify all values
x̄ = 8.5 days | σ = 3.2 days | n = 64 | Confidence Level = 95%
Find Z critical value
α = 1 − 0.95 = 0.05 | α/2 = 0.025
Z(α/2) = Z(0.025) = 1.960 (from standard normal table)
Calculate Standard Error
SE = σ/√n = 3.2/√64 = 3.2/8 = 0.40
Calculate Margin of Error
ME = Z × SE = 1.960 × 0.40 = 0.784
Construct the Confidence Interval
Lower bound = x̄ − ME = 8.5 − 0.784 = 7.716
Upper bound = x̄ + ME = 8.5 + 0.784 = 9.284
95% CI: (7.716, 9.284)
Interpret
We are 95% confident that the true average recovery time for all patients
is between 7.72 days and 9.28 days.

Example 2: 90% CI for Mean (σ unknown — use t-distribution)

Problem A car dealer samples 16 cars and finds the mean fuel efficiency x̄ = 32.4 mpg with s = 4.8 mpg. Build a 90% CI. (σ is unknown)

Identify values
x̄ = 32.4 | s = 4.8 | n = 16 | df = n−1 = 15 | CL = 90%
Find t critical value
α = 0.10 | α/2 = 0.05 | df = 15
t(0.05, 15) = 1.753 (from t-table)
Calculate Standard Error
SE = s/√n = 4.8/√16 = 4.8/4 = 1.20
Margin of Error
ME = t × SE = 1.753 × 1.20 = 2.104
Confidence Interval
Lower = 32.4 − 2.104 = 30.30
Upper = 32.4 + 2.104 = 34.50
90% CI: (30.30, 34.50)

Example 3: 95% CI for Proportion

Problem In a poll of 400 voters, 220 said they support a new policy. Calculate 95% CI for the true proportion supporting the policy.

Calculate sample proportion
p̂ = 220/400 = 0.55 | q̂ = 1 − 0.55 = 0.45
Check conditions
np̂ = 400 × 0.55 = 220 ≥ 10 ✓ | n(1−p̂) = 400 × 0.45 = 180 ≥ 10 ✓
SE for proportion
SE = √[p̂(1−p̂)/n] = √[0.55×0.45/400] = √[0.2475/400] = √0.000619 = 0.02488
Margin of Error (Z = 1.96 for 95%)
ME = 1.96 × 0.02488 = 0.04877 ≈ 4.88%
Confidence Interval
Lower = 0.55 − 0.0488 = 0.501 (50.1%)
Upper = 0.55 + 0.0488 = 0.599 (59.9%)
95% CI: (50.1%, 59.9%)

How Confidence Level Affects Width

Confidence LevelZ ValueMargin of Error (SE=1)Interpretation
90%1.645±1.645Narrower, less certain
95%1.960±1.960Standard balance
99%2.576±2.576Wider, more certain
99.9%3.291±3.291Very wide, very certain
Trade-off: Higher confidence = wider interval = less precise. You can't have high confidence AND a narrow interval without a larger sample size.

When to Use / When NOT to Use

✓ Use Confidence Intervals When
  • Quantifying uncertainty of an estimate
  • Scientific / academic reporting
  • Decision-making where risk matters
  • Comparing two groups (do CIs overlap?)
  • Sample size is reasonably large
  • Communicating precision to stakeholders
  • Regulatory or clinical submissions
✗ Don't Use CIs When
  • n is very small (CI will be too wide to be useful)
  • You have the full population (no estimation needed)
  • Data is not randomly sampled (biased CI)
  • Conditions not met: np̂ < 10 for proportion CI
  • Quick exploratory analysis (use point estimate)

How These 4 Concepts Connect

Population Data → take random samples → CLT tells us sampling distribution is Normal
↓
Standard Error = how spread out those sample means are (σ/√n)
↓
Point Estimation = our single best guess (x̄, p̂, s²)
↓
Confidence Interval = Point Estimate ± (Z or t) × SE
→ gives us a range that captures the truth with specified probability
